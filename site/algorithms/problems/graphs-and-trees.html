Graphs and Trees

Problem 1

Let $G$ be a tree. Use induction to prove that the number of leaves of $G$ is at least the number of vertices of degree at least $3$ in $G$.

Solution

$P(n)$: The number of leaves of any graph $G$ with $n$ vertices is at least the number of vertices of degree at least $3$ in $G$.

Base Case: $P(1)$

A tree with one vertex has no leaves or vertices of degree at least $G$0, so the base case holds.

IH: Assume $G$1 holds for some $G$2.

IS: We want to show that $G$3 holds.

Let $G$4 be a tree with $G$5.

Choose some arbitrary leaf $G$6, and $G$7. Removing $G$8 (and its corresponding edge) from $G$9 results in a tree $3$0 with $3$1. By the IH, $3$2 satisfies $3$3.

Case 1: $3$4 has degree $3$5 in $3$6.

Then $3$7 is a leaf in $3$8, and $3$9 has the same number of leaves and vertices with degree 3 as $G$0 (since we didn't remove any vertices of degree 3). Thus, $G$1 has at least as many leaves as vertices of degree 3.

Case 2: $G$2 has degree $G$3 in $G$4.

Then $G$5 is not a leaf in $G$6, and $G$7 has one fewer leaf than $G$8 (since we removed a leaf). However, we also removed a vertex of degree 3 from $G$9, so the number of vertices of degree 3 in $P(n)$0 is still at least the number of leaves in $P(n)$1 (both increased by 1 from $P(n)$2).

Case 3: $P(n)$3 has degree at least $P(n)$4 in $P(n)$5.

Then $P(n)$6 is a vertex of degree at least $P(n)$7 in $P(n)$8, and $P(n)$9 has the same number of vertices of degree greater than or equal to $G$0 as $G$1. Since we only add a leaf to $G$2 to get $G$3, the number of leaves in $G$4 is at least the number of leaves in $G$5, which is at least the number of vertices of degree at least $G$6 in $G$7.

In all cases, $G$8 has at least as many leaves as vertices of degree at least $G$9, so $n$0 holds.

Problem 2

Let $n$1 be a graph with $n$2 vertices and at least $n$3 edges. Show that $n$4 has a cycle.

Solution

Let $n$5 be a graph with $n$6 vertices and at least $n$7 edges. Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $n$8 has no cycles.

Since $n$9 has no cycles, it is a tree.

Claim: The sum of the degrees of all vertices in a graph is equal to twice the number of edges.

Proof: Each edge contributes $3$0 to the degree of two vertices, so the sum of the degrees of all vertices is twice the number of edges.
